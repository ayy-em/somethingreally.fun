Must know facts about Amsterdam

Definitely handy to know these prior to traveling here. They also can be entertaining enough to keep you from going to sleep for five more minutes, which you would totally regret the next morning. Anyway, let's get this started! You can click the facts to learn more. Well, you should, but I did not test it on any browsers other than Chrome, so good luck.

Key Stuff Everyone Needs To Know:

  • Population: Amsterdam - 872'000, the Netherlands - 17'180'000 (2018).
  • Timezone: UTC+1 (CET) and UTC+2 (CEST) in the summer. For European part, that is.
  • Official language: Dutch (Nederlands). It's spoken by 24 mln people worldwide as a first language!
  • Currency: Euro. Dutch guilder (euro's predecessor in NL) yielded in favor of it on 1st of January 1999.
  • The Netherlands also have 3 municipalities (with 25k people!) in the Caribbean: Bonaire, Sint Eustatius and Saba.
  • With a GDP of $58'255 per capita, the Netherlands are ranked 13th in the world in that regard.
  • Amsterdam's 17th century canals are on the UNESCO World Heritage List.
  • King's Day (27th of April and yes, there is a King) is the biggest Dutch holiday of the year.
  • Soft drugs are not actually completely legal, however, a non-enforcement policy is in place.

Canals have a total length of over 100 kilometers - they actually became part of UNESCO's World Heritage List in 2010. Side fact: Amsterdam has three times as many bridges as Venice does!

At a stunning 183,9 cm on average for males, the Netherlands is either the tallest nation or shares it with Bosnia and Herzegovina, Montenegro and Denmark, claims this random article.

Even though Dutch is obviously the official language in Amsterdam, most of the residents speak English as well as some natives do. Wikipedia mentions 93% of Dutch people claim to be able to converse freely in English. The share of bilinguals is even higher in the capital and the Dutch version of English even has a name - Dunglish (or steenkolenengels).

Apart from residential houseboats, one can find floating hotels, restaurants and a Houseboat museum! Living in one actually requires a special permit and the wooden boats need to be repainted every three years according to the law currently in place. That way the city protects itself from ugly run-down junkyards in the canals.

Between Dutch, Papiamento (Caribbean thing, official in Aruba and Curacao), West Frisian (actually sounds more like Danish), Dutch Low Saxon (more like a dialect) and Limburgish (also spoken in Belgium), that's more than enough diversity for such a small amount of people.

The colony of New Netherlands was established by the Dutch West India Company in 1624 and used to encompass all of present-day NYC, as well as parts of Long Island, New Jersey (thanks, Dutchies) and Connecticut.
